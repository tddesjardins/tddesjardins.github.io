Technical Work

Roman Data

I am currently working with the Data Management System in the Roman Science Operations Center to help define the Roman data format for the Wide Field Instrument, as well as coordinating efforts to create example/test data for development and scientific validation of the data pipeline. The Data Management Division at STScI is in charge of the software development, and is planning to re-use as much of the James Webb data pipeline as possible; however, there are some important differences between James Webb and Roman that necessitate changes to both the pipeline and the reference files, and this is the focus of my work.

Roman Coordinate Systems

The science instrument aperture file (SIAF) is used by the ground systems as part of guide star tracking as well as populating metadata in science files during the calibration pipeline. I have been leading the development and delivery of the Roman SIAF, including making decisions regarding the user-facing coordinate systems and how they transform to sky- and observatory-based frames.

HST/ACS Reference Files

I spearheaded development of a modernized pipeline for the creation of HST/ACS reference files. This includes dark, bias, and sink pixel files used by the science calibration pipeline. This work was done using Python 3 and a MySQL database server, along with a Flask-powered dashboard for generating diagnostic reports.


ACS Polarimetry

I worked on recalibrating the ACS/WFC polarizing filters and updating inconsistencies in the documentation. I also took the initiative to develop code in the ACS Tools Python package to simplify the calculation of polarization properties measured from ACS photometry. With Dean Hines, I co-led the effort to investigate the possibility of using ACS as a spectropolarimeter.

ACS/WFC Calibration Projects

I have been involved in various calibration-related projects to support the ACS Wide Field Channel. These projects have covered many aspects of the ACS/WFC CCDs, and include: leading the development of an absolute gain monitor program that now executes every year, investigating the read noise history (and anomalies) of the detectors, and adivising on the efficacy of the monthly CCD annealing program.

Hubble Help Desk & Documentation

I previously served as the Hubble Space Telescope Help Desk Lead during the early transition to using the Service Now platform. In this role, I organized the general help desk staffing and coordinated with the various instrument help desks, as well as my counterpart on the James Webb Space Telescope. I was also heavily involved in modernizing the Hubble documentation to the HDox documentation system, while further updating many aspects of the HST and ACS Data Handbooks to remove out of date information (e.g., IRAF). I also initially organized development of Jupyter notebooks as examples of ACS data calibration/analysis and assisted in the development of notebooks for AstroDrizzle.

Science Interests

My science background focuses on extragalactic observational astronomy, and I have mainly used X-ray imaging spectroscopy and optical imaging data. I have extensive experience using the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) and Wide Field Planetary Camera 2 (WFPC2) instruments on the Hubble Space Telescope, as well as the Advanced CCD Imaging Spectrometer (ACIS) instrument on the Chandra X-ray Observatory, and several ground-based observing facilities.

A complete listing of scientific publications I have been involved with can be found on the NASA ADS.
My Ph.D. thesis can be found at the University of Western Ontario thesis archive.

Active Galactic Nuclei

Supermassive black holes in the centers of galaxies are the most powerful persistent sources of energy in the Universe. Using multi-wavelength observations, we can begin to untangle their mysteries and peer into the engines in the hearts of galaxies. As an undergraduate, I examined optical SDSS AGN variability and its relationship with black hole mass calculated from SDSS spectroscopy. In my Ph.D. thesis, I also identified AGN candidates in the Coma Cluster using X-ray, optical, infrared, and radio selection criteria.

Environmental Galaxy Evolution

The environments of galaxies have a significant impact on their evolution, from galactic canabalism to major mergers with nearby companions to ram pressure stripping in cluster halos. The effects of these processes over cosmic time shape our view of the nearby Universe, and understanding them is key to understanding both the past and future of galaxies. In my Ph.D. thesis, I examined the diffuse X-ray emission around and between galaxies in compact groups. As a postdoc, I generated photometric catalogs of galaxies in the ESO Distant Cluster Survey (EDisCS) using multiple ground-based observatories.

X-ray Binaries

X-ray binaries consist of a star and a compact object in a system. Relationships have been observed between population of X-ray binaries in galaxies and the galaxy stellar mass (low-mass binaries) as well as the star formation rate (high mass binaries). As a masters student, I examined these relationships in nearby, face-on spiral galaxies using Chandra ACIS and Hubble ACS observations to search for probable optical counterparts to X-ray point sources. This helped to disentangle the low- and high-mass X-ray binary populations for a more accurate analysis of these scaling relationships in other galaxies.
